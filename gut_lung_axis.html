Case Presentation

🩺 24-year-old with chronic cough & loose, greasy stools

A 24-year-old presents with a years-long history of productive cough, recurrent chest infections, and progressive breathlessness. Since childhood they have had bulky, foul-smelling, oily stools that float, along with poor weight gain despite a good appetite. A sibling died in infancy of "lung problems." On exam: low BMI, finger clubbing, and coarse crackles at both lung bases.

Your Call

❓ What is the most likely multisystem diagnosis?

Pick one option. You'll get the answer and the gut–lung axis reasoning straight after.

✅ Cystic Fibrosis (CFTR-related disease)

The unifying diagnosis is cystic fibrosis. A single defective CFTR chloride channel explains every system at once: thick airway secretions cause recurrent infection, bronchiectasis, and chronic Pseudomonas colonization; thick pancreatic secretions block enzyme delivery, causing exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (low fecal elastase, steatorrhea, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency) and, over time, CF-related diabetes (raised glucose). The elevated sweat chloride (88 mmol/L) is the clincher, and the family history fits an autosomal-recessive condition.

The gut–lung axis link: CF is a model of bidirectional gut–lung crosstalk. CFTR dysfunction creates dysbiosis and an abnormal, inflamed gut, while the lungs suffer chronic infection. Pancreatic insufficiency and malnutrition worsen lung outcomes, and the altered gut microbiome shapes systemic and airway immunity. Treating the gut (enzyme replacement, nutrition, vitamins) measurably improves lung trajectory — the two organs are managed as one connected system.
Why not the other options?
  • α₁-antitrypsin deficiency: causes basal (lower-lobe) emphysema + liver disease — but here the AAT level is normal, the disease is upper-lobe bronchiectasis, and there's no liver failure or pancreatic insufficiency.
  • Eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA): features asthma, marked eosinophilia, and vasculitic GI/skin/nerve disease — not steatorrhea, high sweat chloride, or childhood-onset malabsorption.
  • Common variable immunodeficiency (CVID): gives recurrent sinopulmonary infection and GI disease via low immunoglobulins — but it doesn't raise sweat chloride or cause pancreatic exocrine insufficiency.
  • Whipple disease: a gut infection (T. whipplei) with arthralgia and malabsorption that can involve the lung — but it lacks the high sweat chloride, bronchiectasis pattern, and genetic/family picture seen here.
